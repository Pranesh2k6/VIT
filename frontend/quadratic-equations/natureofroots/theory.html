üå≥ Nature of Roots

The discriminant, $D = b^2 - 4ac$, is our guide! Its sign tells us exactly what kind of roots we'll find.

üß© Case 1: $D > 0$ (Discriminant is Positive)

If the discriminant is greater than zero ($D > 0$), the quadratic equation $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$ has **two distinct real roots**.

The roots are given by $x = \frac{-b + \sqrt{D}}{2a}$ and $x = \frac{-b - \sqrt{D}}{2a}$. Since $\sqrt{D}$ is a real number, both roots are real and different.

Positive D means two separate landing spots on the real number line!

‚öîÔ∏è Case 2: $D = 0$ (Discriminant is Zero)

If the discriminant is equal to zero ($D = 0$), the quadratic equation has **one real root** (or sometimes called **two equal real roots**).

The quadratic formula becomes $x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{0}}{2a} = \frac{-b}{2a}$. Both solutions converge to the same single value.

Zero D? Perfect landing! The parabola just touches the x-axis at one single point.

üßó Case 3: $D < 0$ (Discriminant is Negative)

If the discriminant is less than zero ($D < 0$), the quadratic equation has **two complex conjugate roots** (and no real roots).

The quadratic formula involves $\sqrt{D}$, which is $\sqrt{\text{negative number}} = i\sqrt{|D|}$. The roots are $x = \frac{-b \pm i\sqrt{|D|}}{2a}$. These roots are complex numbers of the form $p + iq$ and $p - iq$, where $p = -b/2a$ and $q = \sqrt{|D|}/2a$.

Negative D takes us into the complex realm! The roots come in pairs, like reflections across the real axis.
