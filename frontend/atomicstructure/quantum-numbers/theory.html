Bohr's orbits weren't quite right. Electrons live in fuzzy 'orbitals' described by quantum numbers!

üß© Concept 1: Schr√∂dinger Equation & Orbitals

The modern description of the electron comes from the Schr√∂dinger Wave Equation. We don't solve it in JEE, but its solutions ($\psi$, wave functions) describe regions of space around the nucleus where there is a high probability (often 90%) of finding an electron. These probability regions are called **atomic orbitals**.

‚öîÔ∏è Concept 2: The Four Quantum Numbers

Four quantum numbers act like an "address" specifying the state of an electron:

  • Principal (n): Defines the main **energy level** or shell. $n = 1, 2, 3, ...$. Higher $n$ means higher energy and generally further from the nucleus.
  • Azimuthal/Angular Momentum (l): Defines the **shape** of the orbital and the subshell. $l = 0, 1, 2, ..., (n-1)$.
    • $l=0 \rightarrow$ s orbital (Spherical)
    • $l=1 \rightarrow$ p orbital (Dumbbell)
    • $l=2 \rightarrow$ d orbital (Complex shapes)
    • $l=3 \rightarrow$ f orbital (Even more complex)
  • Magnetic (m or m_l): Defines the **orientation** of the orbital in space within a subshell. $m_l = -l, -l+1, ..., 0, ..., +l-1, +l$. The number of possible $m_l$ values ($2l+1$) tells you how many orbitals are in that subshell (e.g., one s orbital, three p orbitals, five d orbitals).
  • Spin (s or m_s): Defines the intrinsic angular momentum or **spin** of the electron. It can only have two values: $m_s = +1/2$ (spin up $\uparrow$) or $m_s = -1/2$ (spin down $\downarrow$).

Shell (n) $\rightarrow$ Subshell (l) $\rightarrow$ Orbital (m_l) $\rightarrow$ Spin (m_s)! That's the electron's full cosmic address!

üßó Concept 3: Orbital Shapes & Nodes

Visualizing the probability regions gives us shapes:

  • s orbitals ($l=0$): Spherical.
  • p orbitals ($l=1$): Dumbbell-shaped along the x, y, and z axes ($p_x, p_y, p_z$).
  • d orbitals ($l=2$): Mostly cloverleaf shapes ($d_{xy}, d_{yz}, d_{zx}, d_{x^2-y^2}$) plus one dumbbell with a torus ($d_{z^2}$).

A **node** is a region where the probability of finding the electron is zero. There are **radial nodes** (spheres at certain distances from the nucleus, number = $n-l-1$) and **angular nodes** (planes or cones passing through the nucleus, number = $l$).
